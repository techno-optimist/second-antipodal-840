\begin{abstract}
We exhibit a new antipodal kissing configuration of $840$ points ($420$
lines through the origin of $\RR^{12}$ at pairwise angles at least
$60^\circ$): the $132$ root lines $(e_a\pm e_b)/\sqrt2$ together with $288$
half-integer lines with entries $\pm\tfrac1{2\sqrt2}$ on eight-coordinate
supports, in the even sign class, spread over $15$ partial cells. All
$\binom{420}{2}=87{,}990$ pairwise inner products are certified
$|\langle u,v\rangle|\le 1/2$ in exact arithmetic by two independent
routes, of which the stdlib checker ships with this note's repository;
$20{,}464$ pairs ($23.3\%$) meet the bound with equality, so the
configuration is extremely rigid. A packing-independent counting theorem
proves it is \emph{not} isometric to the classical $840$-point
configuration ($24$ axis vectors plus $816$ weight-$4$ vectors over a
maximum quadruple packing), nor to any configuration of $12$ axes plus $51$
whole weight-$4$ cells: the new inner-product histogram
$\{0\!:\!35{,}142,\ \tfrac14\!:\!32{,}384,\ \tfrac12\!:\!20{,}464\}$ forces
a block-pair statistic $P_2\ge 564$ while matching it requires $P_2=563$.
An exact $\QQ(\sqrt2)$ rotation certificate nevertheless shows the new
configuration is a blockwise $45^\circ$ rotation of a $12$-axes $+$
$47$-whole $+$ $8$-half weight-$4$-cell configuration: it evades the known
$420$ but not the integer weight-$4$ world, and is (to our knowledge) the
first $420$-line configuration that uses \emph{partial} cells. We prove a
slice-closure theorem, whose single computational input is a CP-SAT
\textsc{optimal} certificate: for every one of the $10{,}395$ pairings of the $12$
coordinates into $6$ pairs, the root lines together with the pair-aligned
half-integer cells embed isometrically into the integer world, so such
``slice'' configurations cap at exactly $420$ lines and pair-aligned
half-integer configurations cap at exactly $288$. An exact CP-SAT ladder
then proves (\textsc{optimal} at every node, full orbit enumeration through
level $3$) that any $289$-line half-integer configuration --- which would
yield $421$ lines, $842$ points, and a world-record kissing configuration
--- must use at least $4$ cells foreign to \emph{every} pairing; extensive
exact search at levels $4$--$6$ and beyond found none. We state the honest
scope precisely: $\alpha(\text{half-integer world})=288$ is strongly
evidenced but not globally proven. A structural attack on the remaining gap
then yields four theorems: an exact structure lemma for conflicting cell
pairs (the bipartite conflict graph at support overlaps $5/6/7$ is
$16\times K_{4,4}$, $32\times K_{2,2}$, and the folded-$7$-cube unit-ball
bipartite graph); a closure of the even-overlap side proving (given the
same solver-certified slice cap) that the
missing \emph{odd-overlap lemma} is \emph{equivalent} to
$\alpha(U)=288$; an LP barrier theorem --- no family of valid occupancy
inequalities with at most $109$ positive-support cells each (which includes
every pair, triple, localization, slice, and ladder cut we possess) can
certify $\alpha(U)\le 289$; and a star reduction
$\alpha(U_{12})\le 3\,\alpha(U_{11})$ that passes the barrier and is
exactly tight on the witness, reducing both the global closure and the
record hunt to a single question in a world three times smaller: is
$\alpha(U_{11})\le 96$? (Answered in the negative in the v1.1 postscript,
Appendix~\ref{sec:postscript}.) Finally, a $\vartheta$-certified
architecture sweep closes every enumerable frame family in dimensions $11$
and $12$ far below the record thresholds and exposes a structural
anti-correlation: the richer the frame, the thinner the weight-$4$ base it
leaves behind. Whether $\RR^{12}$ contains an antipodal $842$-point kissing
configuration ($421$ lines; our prior LP ceiling is $614$ lines
\cite{RussellLines26}) remains open.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

The kissing number $k(d)$ is the maximum number of unit spheres that can
simultaneously touch a central unit sphere in $\RR^d$; equivalently, the
maximum number of unit vectors with pairwise angles at least $60^\circ$.
In $\RR^{12}$ the classical record configuration has $840$ points
(\cite[Table~1.5]{SPLAG}; the kissing-number survey \cite{BDM15} confirms
that the $n\le 24$ lower-bound table, including $840$ at $n=12$, follows
SPLAG Table~1.5), and the record was recently raised to $k(12)\ge 841$ by
Takhanov, Assylbekov and Yun \cite{TAY26}, who found room for an $841$st
sphere by a global rebuild of an $840$-point base; the best upper bound is
$k(12)\le 1355$ \cite{deLaatLeijenhorst24}. Neighboring dimension $11$ has
seen the same burst of computer-search activity ($k(11)\ge 593$ by
AlphaEvolve \cite{AlphaEvolve25}, then $k(11)\ge 604$ by Bianchi et
al.~\cite{Bianchi26}).

A kissing configuration is \emph{antipodal} if it is closed under
$x\mapsto-x$; antipodal configurations of $2N$ points are the same thing as
systems of $N$ lines through the origin with pairwise angles at least
$60^\circ$ (coherence $|\cos|\le 1/2$). The $840$-point classical record
configuration is antipodal ($420$ lines); the $841$-point record of
\cite{TAY26} is \emph{not}: an odd-cardinality set cannot be closed under
$x\mapsto-x$, and our direct check of coordinates recovered from the
authors' published Gram data --- a floating-point side observation, not
part of this note's certificate set --- finds no exactly antipodal pair
(minimum pairwise cosine $-0.99999999$; the global rebuild retains
hundreds of \emph{near}-antipodal pairs but exact antipodality appears to
be broken).
An antipodal configuration with $842$ points is exactly a system of $421$
lines at coherence $1/2$; since $842>841$, \emph{any} such system would be
a world-record kissing configuration. In our predecessor note
\cite{RussellLines26} we proved the two-point LP ceiling
$N_{60}(12)\le 614$ in exact rational arithmetic --- so an antipodal record
attempt at $421$ lines is not excluded by any known ceiling; the gap
between the realized $420$ and the LP $614$ is enormous. The present note
documents what we found when we went looking for the $421$st line.

The contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A new $420$-line configuration} (Section~\ref{sec:config}),
found by independent-set search over an exactly generated mixed-alphabet
conflict graph and verified twice in exact arithmetic
(Theorem~\ref{thm:exists}). Its composition --- $132$ root lines plus
$288$ half-integer lines over $15$ \emph{partial} cells, zero axes, zero
weight-$4$ lines --- is structurally unlike the classical $420$.
\item \textbf{A novelty theorem} (Section~\ref{sec:novelty}): a
packing-independent counting argument (Theorem~\ref{thm:novelty}) proving
non-isometry to the classical configuration and to every configuration of
its combinatorial type. The proof is a five-line double count; no solver
is involved.
\item \textbf{A rotation certificate and slice-closure theorems}
(Section~\ref{sec:rotation}): an exact $\QQ(\sqrt2)$ isometry mapping the
new configuration into the integer weight-$4$ world
(Theorem~\ref{thm:rotation}), and a closure theorem for every coordinate
pairing (Theorem~\ref{thm:slice}) showing that ``slice'' configurations cap
at exactly $420$ lines. The new configuration therefore ties the record
\emph{within} a capped world; any improvement must mix across slices.
\item \textbf{An exact exclusion ladder} (Section~\ref{sec:ladder}):
CP-SAT proofs, \textsc{optimal} at every node with full orbit enumeration
through level $3$, that a $289$-line half-integer configuration must sit at
least $4$ cells foreign to every one of the $10{,}395$ pairings
(Theorem~\ref{thm:ladder}), together with the (negative) outcome of
extensive exact search beyond the proven levels. We state exactly what is
proven and what is only searched.
\item \textbf{The odd-overlap frontier} (Section~\ref{sec:odd}): an exact
structure lemma for every conflicting cell pair
(Theorem~\ref{thm:structure}) with machine-tight pair and triple caps; a
proof that configurations whose occupied cells overlap pairwise
\emph{evenly} cap at $288$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:evenclosure}), repairing a
false cell-level claim in our own campaign roadmap; the resulting
equivalence \emph{odd-overlap lemma $\Leftrightarrow$ $\alpha(U)=288$}
(Corollary~\ref{cor:equiv}); an LP barrier theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:barrier}) proving that no aggregation of small-support
valid inequalities --- including every cut family in this note --- can
certify $\alpha(U)\le 289$; and a star reduction
(Theorem~\ref{thm:star}) to the $10{,}560$-line $U_{11}$ world that passes
the barrier and is exactly tight on the witness. The paper's headline open
problem becomes: \emph{is $\alpha(U_{11})\le 96$?}
\item \textbf{An architecture-exhaustion sweep} (Section~\ref{sec:sweep}):
$\vartheta$-certified and ILP-exact closures of every enumerable frame
family we could write down in dimensions $11$ and $12$, all far below the
record thresholds, and the structural anti-correlation between frame
richness and weight-$4$ base capacity that explains the pattern.
\end{enumerate}
Section~\ref{sec:disc} collects open problems, chief among them the
$U_{11}$ question $\alpha(U_{11})\le 96$ --- to which Section~\ref{sec:odd}
reduces both the global closure of the half-integer world \emph{and} the
antipodal record hunt --- and the status of $842$.

Throughout, ``certified'' means: recomputed from the raw witness by the
stdlib-only checker in exact integer or $\QQ(\sqrt2)$ arithmetic
(\texttt{make verify}, Appendix~\ref{sec:repro}); no floating point on the
certified path, and no claimed value echoed from a file. Solver statuses
(CP-SAT \textsc{optimal}, certified $\vartheta$, KaMIS incumbents) are
reported as such.

\section{Four alphabets and one conflict graph}\label{sec:alphabets}

All configurations in this note live in the union of four line alphabets in
$\RR^{12}$, each with an integer representative:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllll@{}}
\toprule
alphabet & unit representative & integer rep.\ $g$ & $N=\langle g,g\rangle$ & \#lines \\
\midrule
$A$ (axes) & $e_i$ & $e_i$ & $1$ & $12$ \\
$W_2$ (roots) & $(e_a\pm e_b)/\sqrt2$ & $e_a\pm e_b$ & $2$ & $132$ \\
$W_4$ (weight-$4$) & $\tfrac12\sum_{i\in B}s_ie_i$, $|B|=4$, $s_i=\pm1$ & $\sum_{i\in B}s_ie_i$ & $4$ & $3960$ \\
$U$ (half-integer) & $\tfrac1{2\sqrt2}\sum_{i\in H}s_ie_i$, $|H|=8$, even & $\sum_{i\in H}s_ie_i$ & $8$ & $31{,}680$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Here $B$ ranges over the $\binom{12}{4}=495$ four-point supports ($8$ lines
each, sign vectors up to negation), and $H$ over the $\binom{12}{8}=495$
eight-point supports (``cells''). In the $U$ alphabet we keep only the
\emph{even sign class} (an even number of minus signs); negation preserves
sign parity on an $8$-support, so each cell carries
$2^8/2\cdot\tfrac12=64$ lines and $|U|=495\cdot 64=31{,}680$.

Two lines with integer representatives $g_1,g_2$ of norms $N_1,N_2$ are
\emph{in conflict} (angle below $60^\circ$) iff
\begin{equation}\label{eq:predicate}
4\,\langle g_1,g_2\rangle^2 \;>\; N_1 N_2 ,
\end{equation}
a pure big-integer predicate; a legal configuration is an independent set
of the conflict graph. The full graph on
$12+3960+132+31{,}680=35{,}784$ vertices has $40{,}130{,}904$ edges. Some
structural facts, each recomputed exactly from \eqref{eq:predicate} during
graph construction (artifact \texttt{K2\_stats.json}, predicate-validation
mismatches $0$; the repository README reproduces the full alphabet-pair
edge census):
\begin{itemize}
\item $W_2$ is internally conflict-free ($0$ $W_2$--$W_2$ edges) and
conflict-free against all of $U$ ($0$ $W_2$--$U$ edges); each root line
conflicts with exactly the two axes in its support ($264$ $A$--$W_2$
edges), and with weight-$4$ lines as dictated by \eqref{eq:predicate}
($23{,}760$ $W_4$--$W_2$ edges).
\item Two $U$-lines in cells $H,H'$ with $s=|H\cap H'|$ common coordinates
have $\langle u,u'\rangle=\tfrac18\sum_{i\in H\cap H'}s_is_i'$, so they
conflict iff $\bigl|\sum_{i\in H\cap H'}s_is_i'\bigr|\ge 5$; all realized
$|\cos|$ values lie in $\{0,\tfrac18,\tfrac14,\tfrac38,\tfrac12\}$.
Cell pairs with $s=4$ are conflict-free; the census of cell pairs is
$s{=}4\!:17{,}325$, $s{=}5\!:55{,}440$, $s{=}6\!:41{,}580$,
$s{=}7\!:7{,}920$ (total $\binom{495}{2}=122{,}265$). A conflicting
cell pair induces $256/128/512$ conflict edges among its $64\times64$
line pairs at $s=5/6/7$ respectively (the structure lemma,
Theorem~\ref{thm:structure}), and at most $64$ lines can be chosen
from the two cells jointly (the pair cap, Corollary~\ref{cor:kappa}).
\item The induced $U$-world graph has $31{,}680$ vertices,
$23{,}569{,}920$ edges, and is $1488$-regular and vertex-transitive; its
largest adjacency eigenvalue is $1488$ (the regularity, exact), and its
smallest is $-50$ by numerical eigensolve
(\texttt{V2\_DECISION\_SUMMARY.json}; we have not certified
$\lambda_{\min}$ exactly, and it enters only the avowedly loose Hoffman row
of Table~\ref{tab:loose}).
\end{itemize}

Because coherence is scale-invariant, mixing the norm-$2$, norm-$4$ and
norm-$8$ shells is legitimate --- the mixed graph is exactly the object a
cross-shell (``grand mixed'') search should explore, and to our knowledge
it had not been searched before.

\section{The configuration and its verification}\label{sec:config}

\begin{theorem}[a second $420$]\label{thm:exists}
There exists an antipodal kissing configuration of $840$ points in
$\RR^{12}$, i.e.\ a system of $420$ lines with pairwise $|\cos|\le 1/2$,
consisting of all $132$ root lines $W_2$ together with $288$ half-integer
lines from $U$, distributed over the $15$ cells of
Table~\ref{tab:cells} ($24$ lines in each of $3$ cells, $20$ in each of
$6$, $16$ in each of $6$). All $87{,}990$ line pairs satisfy the coherence
bound exactly, with $20{,}464$ pairs at $|\cos|=\tfrac12$ exactly, and the
multiset of $|\cos|$ values is
\[
\Bigl\{\,0^{\,35142},\ \bigl(\tfrac14\bigr)^{32384},\
\bigl(\tfrac12\bigr)^{20464}\Bigr\}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\texttt{make verify}. The witness \texttt{certs/config420.json} lists the
$420$ integer representatives; the checker recomputes every pairwise
predicate \eqref{eq:predicate} in big-integer arithmetic (independently
re-verified in exact $\QQ(\sqrt2)$ by the campaign adjudicator, a second
route that is not part of this repository's certificate set:
$0$ violations on both), together with the composition, the cell
distribution, and the displayed histogram. No claimed count is echoed:
histogram and margins are recomputed from the vectors.
\end{proof}

\emph{Provenance (not part of the certificate).} The configuration was
found by KaMIS \cite{KaMIS17} (weighted branch-and-reduce, $7200$\,s) as a
maximum-independent-set incumbent on the full $35{,}784$-vertex mixed
graph. The incumbent contains zero axes and zero weight-$4$ lines --- the
search abandoned the classical architecture entirely. Two facts make the
object remarkable before any novelty argument: $23.3\%$ of its pairs are at
zero margin (tight $|\cos|=\tfrac12$), and its $288$ half-integer lines
occupy $4.5$ cells' worth of capacity spread over $15$ partial cells, so
within the half-integer world \emph{mixing beats whole-cell packing} ---
the opposite of the regime we had established in dimension~$11$.

Since the $132$ root lines are conflict-free among themselves and against
all of $U$, this architecture reduces cleanly:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reduction}
\alpha\bigl(W_2\cup U\bigr)\;=\;132+\alpha(U),
\end{equation}
and the record condition ``$421$ lines'' is exactly $\alpha(U)\ge 289$.
The rest of the note is organized around that single question.

\section{Novelty: a packing-independent counting theorem}\label{sec:novelty}

The classical $420$ consists of the $12$ axes plus $51$ \emph{whole}
weight-$4$ cells over a maximum quadruple packing: $D(3,4,12)=51$ is the
maximum number of $4$-subsets of a $12$-set with every $3$-subset covered
at most once. In constant-weight-code terms this is $A(12,4,4)=51$, listed
as exact in the standard tables \cite{BSSS90}; quadruple systems go back to
Hanani \cite{Hanani60}, and \cite{HartmanPhelps92} surveys the packing
theory. Our artifacts independently cross-check the value by exact ILP
(HiGHS, integer optimal). With $51$ blocks, $12+8\cdot51=420$ lines and
$24+16\cdot51=840$ points.
The inner-product histogram is an isometry invariant, and the classical
configuration's histogram, recomputed exactly from an explicit $51$-block
packing (artifact \texttt{modular420\_invariants.json}; a campaign
artifact, not part of this repository's certificate set), is
$\{0\!:\!35{,}238,\ \tfrac14\!:\!32{,}256,\ \tfrac12\!:\!20{,}496\}$ ---
already different from the new configuration's histogram, which settles
non-isometry to that specific object. The following theorem is much
stronger: no configuration of the classical \emph{type} realizes the new
histogram, whatever packing it is built on.

\begin{theorem}[novelty]\label{thm:novelty}
No line configuration consisting of the $12$ axis lines together with $51$
whole weight-$4$ cells (over any legal family of $51$ blocks) has
inner-product histogram
$\{0\!:\!35{,}142,\ \tfrac14\!:\!32{,}384,\ \tfrac12\!:\!20{,}464\}$.
Consequently the configuration of Theorem~\ref{thm:exists} is not
isometric to the classical $420$, nor to any axes-plus-whole-cells
configuration.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Every line of such a configuration has an integer representative of norm
$N\in\{1,4\}$, so every pairwise $|\cos|$ is $|d|/\sqrt{N_1N_2}$ with
$d\in\ZZ$ and $\sqrt{N_1N_2}\in\{1,2,4\}$: a multiple of $\tfrac14$, and
legality restricts to $\{0,\tfrac14,\tfrac12\}$.

Let the blocks be $B_1,\dots,B_{51}$, let $r_i$ be the number of blocks
containing point $i$, and let $P_1$ (resp.\ $P_2$) be the number of block
pairs sharing exactly $1$ (resp.\ $2$) points. Legality forces every block
pair to share at most $2$ points: two \emph{whole} cells on blocks sharing
$3$ points contain a sign-aligned triple, realizing $|\cos|=\tfrac34$.
Counting each $|\cos|$ class (a finite check per case; the checker
recomputes it):
axis--axis pairs contribute $0$'s; axis--cell pairs contribute
$|\cos|=\tfrac12$ exactly when the axis point lies in the block ($8$ lines
per block): $8\sum_i r_i$ pairs; each whole cell internally contributes
$16$ pairs at $\tfrac12$ and $12$ at $0$; a block pair sharing $2$ points
contributes $32$ pairs at $\tfrac12$ and $32$ at $0$; a block pair sharing
$1$ point contributes all $64$ pairs at $\tfrac14$; disjoint block pairs
contribute $0$'s. Hence
\[
n_{1/4}=64\,P_1,
\qquad
n_{1/2}=8\sum_i r_i+16\cdot 51+32\,P_2 .
\]
Since every block has $4$ points, $\sum_i r_i=4\cdot51=204$ always, so
matching the stated histogram requires
\[
P_1=\tfrac{32384}{64}=506,
\qquad
P_2=\tfrac{20464-1632-816}{32}=563 .
\]
Double-counting incidences of points in block pairs,
$\sum_i\binom{r_i}{2}=P_1+2P_2=506+1126=1632$. By convexity with
$\sum_i r_i=204$ over $12$ points (mean $17$),
$\sum_i\binom{r_i}{2}\ge 12\binom{17}{2}=1632$, with equality iff
$r_i=17$ for every $i$. So $r_i\equiv 17$ is forced.

Now let $\lambda_p$ be the number of blocks containing the pair
$p\subset[12]$. Blocks through a fixed pair $p$ have pairwise-disjoint
$2$-point remainders in the other $10$ points (two blocks through $p$
sharing a third point would share $3$ points), so $\lambda_p\le 5$. Fix a
point $i$: each block through $i$ contains $3$ pairs through $i$, so
$\sum_{j\ne i}\lambda_{ij}=3r_i=51$, a sum of $11$ values in
$\{0,\dots,5\}$. The convex functional $\sum_{j\ne i}\binom{\lambda_{ij}}2$
is minimized at the most-equal admissible vector $(5^7,4^4)$:
$7\cdot10+4\cdot6=94$. A block pair sharing exactly $2$ points shares
exactly one pair, so $\sum_p\binom{\lambda_p}2=P_2$, and summing the
per-point inequality over the $12$ points counts each pair twice:
\[
2P_2=\sum_i\sum_{j\ne i}\binom{\lambda_{ij}}2\;\ge\;12\cdot 94=1128,
\qquad\text{so } P_2\ge 564 .
\]
This contradicts $P_2=563$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The bound is tight in the right way: the classical configuration itself has
$(P_1,P_2)=(504,564)$ with $r_i\equiv17$ and pair degrees
$\lambda\in\{4^{24},5^{42}\}$, sitting exactly on the convexity floor.
The new histogram misses it by one.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[weaker invariants fail]
The novelty is invisible to cruder tests. The new configuration realizes
only the classical $|\cos|$ support $\{0,\tfrac14,\tfrac12\}$ --- the
odd-eighth values $\tfrac18,\tfrac38$ available to the half-integer
alphabet are never used (every cohabiting cell pair has even overlap), so
the ``odd-eighth certificate'' one might hope for does not fire. Per-line
degree profiles also fail: the new configuration contains exactly $12$
lines with profile $(n_0,n_{1/4},n_{1/2})=(283,0,136)$, \emph{identical}
to the classical configuration's axis lines at replication $17$. The
counting theorem is what remains after these invariants collapse.
\end{remark}

\section{The rotation certificate and slice closure}\label{sec:rotation}

Novelty notwithstanding, the new configuration is not far from the integer
world --- it is a rotation of a mixed whole/partial-cell integer
configuration, a fact we certify exactly.

For a \emph{pairing} $P$ of the $12$ coordinates into $6$ unordered pairs
(there are $11!!=10{,}395$ pairings), let $T_P\in O(12)$ be the blockwise
rotation by $45^\circ$ in each of the $6$ coordinate planes: on the plane
of the pair $(a,b)$,
\[
T_P\,e_a=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(e_a+e_b),
\qquad
T_P\,e_b=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(-e_a+e_b).
\]
Call an $8$-support $H$ \emph{$P$-aligned} if $H$ is a union of $4$ pairs
of $P$ (equivalently its $4$-point complement is a union of $2$ pairs);
each pairing has $\binom64=15$ aligned supports.

\begin{theorem}[rotation certificate]\label{thm:rotation}
Let $P_0=\{(0,9),(1,2),(3,4),(5,11),(6,7),(8,10)\}$. Then
$T_{P_0}$ maps the configuration of Theorem~\ref{thm:exists} exactly
--- certified in $\QQ(\sqrt2)$ arithmetic, line by line --- onto a
configuration in the integer world $A\cup W_4$ consisting of the $12$ axis
lines plus $408$ weight-$4$ lines over $55$ supports: $47$ whole cells
($8$ lines) and $8$ half cells ($4$ lines).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\texttt{make verify} replays the isometry: the checker applies the
explicit $T_{P_0}$ (entries in $\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\ZZ$) to each of the
$420$ integer representatives, working in $\ZZ[\sqrt2]$, and verifies that
every image is an axis or weight-$4$ line, then recomputes the support
census $47\cdot8+8\cdot4=408$, $12+408=420$
(certificate \texttt{certs/rotation.json}).
\end{proof}

So the new configuration is Hanani-capped in disguise: up to isometry it
lives inside the integer weight-$4$ world --- but as the first known
$420$ using \emph{partial} cells ($8$ half cells), which is exactly what
Theorem~\ref{thm:novelty} says it must do to avoid the classical type.
The mechanism generalizes to every pairing:

\begin{theorem}[slice closure]\label{thm:slice}
Fix any pairing $P$ and let the \emph{$P$-slice} be the line family
consisting of all $132$ root lines together with all $15\cdot64=960$
half-integer lines in $P$-aligned cells. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] $T_P$ maps the $P$-slice isometrically into the integer
world $A\cup W_4$;
\item[(b)] every legal configuration of $P$-aligned half-integer lines has
at most $288$ lines, and $288$ is achieved;
\item[(c)] every legal configuration inside the $P$-slice has at most
$420$ lines, and $420$ is achieved.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(a) Root lines on a pair of $P$ map to axes:
$T_P\bigl((e_a+e_b)/\sqrt2\bigr)=e_b$ and
$T_P\bigl((e_a-e_b)/\sqrt2\bigr)=e_a$ for $(a,b)\in P$ ($12$ lines);
root lines across two pairs map to weight-$4$ lines (four entries
$\pm\tfrac12$). For a half-integer line $u$ on a $P$-aligned support, each
of its $4$ pairing planes carries coordinates
$(x,y)\in\{\pm\tfrac1{2\sqrt2}\}^2$, and in-plane
\[
T_P:\ (x,y)\ \longmapsto\
\Bigl(\tfrac{x-y}{\sqrt2},\ \tfrac{x+y}{\sqrt2}\Bigr),
\]
where exactly one of $x\pm y$ vanishes and the other is $\pm\tfrac1{\sqrt2}$;
so the image has exactly one entry $\pm\tfrac12$ per plane: four entries
$\pm\tfrac12$ on a $4$-support, a weight-$4$ line. An orthogonal map
preserves all inner products, and distinct lines map to distinct lines.

(b) Upper bound: the $P$-aligned half-integer world is a $960$-vertex
induced subgraph of the $U$-conflict graph; an exact CP-SAT \cite{CPSAT}
solve certifies its maximum independent set to be $288$
(status \textsc{optimal}; receipt
\texttt{certs/receipts/result\_slice\_only.json}).
The symmetric group $S_{12}$ permutes coordinates, acts transitively on
pairings, and induces isomorphisms of the aligned-cell graphs, so the
computation for one pairing settles all $10{,}395$. Achievability is the
$288$-line witness of Theorem~\ref{thm:exists} (whose $15$ cells are
exactly the $P_0$-aligned supports --- all of them carry lines).

(c) Root lines are conflict-free among themselves and against all of $U$,
so a maximum slice configuration is all $132$ roots plus a maximum
$P$-aligned $U$-configuration: $132+288=420$ by (b), achieved by
Theorem~\ref{thm:exists}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[two routes to the cap]
Part (b) rests on one finite certified-\textsc{optimal} computation. An
alternative, computation-free route runs through (a) plus the maximality of
the classical $840$ for the integer norm-$4$ alphabet
($\alpha(A\cup W_4)=12+8\cdot D(3,4,12)=420$): embedding a $P$-aligned
$289$-configuration plus all $132$ roots into $A\cup W_4$ would give $421$
there. We established that maximality in earlier campaign work (campaign log,
2026-07-01); its formal writeup and receipt are \emph{not} part of this
note's certificate set, which is why the theorem above is stated with the
self-contained CP-SAT route. (A subtlety worth recording: \emph{partial}
$W_4$ cells can share $3$ support points without illegality --- only two
\emph{whole} cells on $3$-point-sharing blocks force a sign-aligned triple
--- so the $A\cup W_4$ cap is genuinely stronger than the naive whole-cell
count and must be proven at line level, as the campaign computation did.)
\end{remark}

The strategic consequence is sharp: within any single slice the record is
unreachable ($420$ exactly), so
\begin{center}
\emph{a $421$-line configuration of this architecture --- equivalently a
$289$-line half-integer configuration, by \eqref{eq:reduction} --- must
mix across slices, i.e.\ use cell pairs with odd overlap
$|H\cap H'|\in\{5,7\}$, the $|\cos|\in\{\tfrac18,\tfrac38\}$ regime.}
\end{center}
The ``i.e.''\ deserves care: escaping every slice and containing an
odd-overlap cell pair are \emph{not} the same condition at cell level, and
the gap is exactly the even-but-unaligned cell families of
Section~\ref{sec:odd}, where the displayed statement is proven outright
(Corollary~\ref{cor:oddpair}).

\section{The odd-overlap frontier: pair structure, even closure, an LP
barrier, and the star reduction}\label{sec:odd}

The ladder localizes a hypothetical $289$; this section reports a direct
structural attack on the gap itself (a dedicated lemma-hunt session,
2026-07-10/11). The attack did not prove the odd-overlap lemma. It
produced something arguably better: an exact description of the local
conflict structure, a closure of the entire even-overlap side that repairs
a hole in our own roadmap, a proof that the odd-overlap lemma is
\emph{equivalent} to the global statement $\alpha(U)=288$, a barrier
theorem showing that every cut family in this note --- the ladder's
included --- is provably insufficient to close it, and a reduction of both
the closure and the record hunt to a single sharp question in a world
three times smaller.

Every claim below carries one of three labels. \textsc{(proven)}: a
complete proof appears here. \textsc{(machine)}: an exhaustive exact
computation --- stdlib-only big-integer branch-and-bound, no solver, no
floats --- cited with its exact content; the scripts and result files ship
with the repository (\texttt{scripts/oddlemma/}) and every such entry
reproduces in seconds. \textsc{(open)}: open, stated as such.

\emph{Notation.} We index a cell by its $4$-point \emph{defect}
$A=[12]\setminus H$ rather than its $8$-point support $H$; cells are the
$\binom{12}{4}=495$ four-subsets of $[12]$. For cells $A\ne B$ put
$t=|A\cap B|$; the common support is exactly
$H\cap H'=[12]\setminus(A\cup B)=:R$ with $r=|R|=4+t$, so $t=0,1,2,3$
corresponds to support overlap $|H\cap H'|=4,5,6,7$. $t=0$ pairs are
conflict-free (``free''), $t\in\{1,3\}$ are the odd-overlap pairs, $t=2$
the even ones. For a configuration $S$ and a cell $A$, $n_A$ denotes the
number of $S$-lines in cell $A$ ($0\le n_A\le 64$).

\subsection{The structure of a conflicting cell pair}

\begin{theorem}[structure lemma]\label{thm:structure}
Let $A,B$ be cells with $t=|A\cap B|\in\{1,2,3\}$, and for a line $u$ in
either cell let its \emph{color} be the restriction of its sign vector to
$R$, taken modulo global sign. Then the bipartite conflict graph between
the $64$ lines of $A$ and the $64$ lines of $B$ is: \textup{(i)} for $t=1$
($r=5$): $16$ color classes of $4$ lines per cell; two lines conflict iff
they have the same color; the graph is $16$ disjoint copies of $K_{4,4}$,
$256$ edges. \textup{(ii)} for $t=2$ ($r=6$): $32$ colors of $2$; $32$
disjoint $K_{2,2}$, $128$ edges. \textup{(iii)} for $t=3$ ($r=7$): the
color map is a bijection from each cell's $64$ lines onto the folded
$7$-cube $\mathbb{F}_2^7/\langle\mathbf 1\rangle$, and two lines conflict
iff their folded Hamming distance is $\le 1$; the graph is the bipartite
unit-ball graph of the folded $7$-cube, $64\cdot 8=512$ edges.
\textsc{(proven}; censuses $256/128/512$ independently
\textsc{machine}-verified, \texttt{scripts/oddlemma/uw.py} self-test, and
recorded in the campaign artifact \texttt{V2\_DECISION\_SUMMARY.json}%
\textsc{)}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $\varepsilon,\varepsilon'$ for sign vectors of lines $u$ (on $H_A$)
and $v$ (on $H_B$). Since $8\langle u,v\rangle=\sum_{i\in R}
\varepsilon_i\varepsilon'_i$ and conflict means $|\cos|>\tfrac12$, the
pair conflicts iff $\bigl|\sum_{i\in R}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon'_i\bigr|
\ge5$. With $d$ the Hamming distance of the two restrictions to $R$, the
sum is $r-2d$, so conflict means $|r-2d|\ge 5$: for $r=5$, $d\in\{0,5\}$;
for $r=6$, $d\in\{0,6\}$; for $r=7$, $d\in\{0,1,6,7\}$. The first two
cases say exactly ``restrictions agree up to global sign on $R$'' (same
color); the third says ``folded distance $\le1$''.

Fiber sizes: a cell's support $H$ contains $R$ with $|H\setminus R|=8-r$.
Given any sign pattern on $R$, the even-parity constraint leaves
$2^{8-r-1}$ completions on $H\setminus R$ when $r<8$, and modding by
global flip pairs the pattern $x$ (with its completions) against $\bar x$
(with the flipped completions); each folded color class
$\{x,\bar x\}$ therefore carries exactly $2^{8-r-1}$ lines of the cell:
$4$ per class for $r=5$ ($16$ classes), $2$ for $r=6$ ($32$ classes), $1$
for $r=7$ ($64$ classes --- a bijection). The folded $7$-cube is
$7$-regular (neighbors of $\{x,\bar x\}$ are the classes $\{x+e_i\}$,
$i\in R$, pairwise distinct), so each line has $1+7=8$ conflict partners
at $t=3$, giving $64\cdot8=512$ edges; the same-color counts give
$16\cdot4\cdot4=256$ and $32\cdot2\cdot2=128$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[pair cap]\label{cor:kappa}
For every conflicting cell pair, $n_A+n_B\le 64$, and $64$ is attained for
every $t\in\{1,2,3\}$. \textsc{(proven)}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$t=1,2$: within one color class the conflict graph is complete bipartite,
so an independent set uses lines of at most one cell per class; each class
holds $4$ (resp.\ $2$) lines of one cell, whence at most $16\cdot4$
(resp.\ $32\cdot2$) $=64$ lines in total. $t=3$: identify both cells with
the folded $7$-cube $V$; an independent set is $S_A\sqcup S_B$ with $S_B
\cap N[S_A]=\emptyset$, so $|S_A|+|S_B|\le|S_A|+64-|N[S_A]|\le 64$ since
$S_A\subseteq N[S_A]$. Tightness: one full cell.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[$t=3$ rigidity]\label{thm:rigid}
If $t=3$ and \emph{both} cells are nonempty, then $n_A+n_B\le 57$, and
$(1,56)$ is attained. By contrast $t=1$ and $t=2$ have no such penalty:
both-nonempty pairs attain $64$.
\textsc{(proven}, with the folded-$7$-cube connectivity input
\textsc{machine}-verified; the exact both-nonempty maxima
$57/64/64$ are also directly \textsc{machine}-computed
(\texttt{stage3.py}, \texttt{stage6.py})\textsc{)}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The folded $7$-cube is vertex-transitive and $7$-connected: connectivity
was verified exactly by unit-capacity max-flow (Menger) over all
non-neighbor pairs from a base vertex, which suffices by transitivity
(\texttt{stage3.py}); it is also an instance of the theorem that the
vertex connectivity of a distance-regular graph equals its valency
\cite{BrouwerKoolen09}. Now let $S_A,S_B\ne\emptyset$ be the two sides of
an independent set. Since $S_B\ne\emptyset$, $N[S_A]\ne V$; then the
boundary $\partial S_A=N[S_A]\setminus S_A$ separates $S_A$ from
$V\setminus N[S_A]$, so $|\partial S_A|\ge 7$ by $7$-connectivity. Hence
$n_A+n_B\le|S_A|+\bigl(64-|S_A|-7\bigr)=57$. For tightness take $S_A$ a
single vertex ($|\partial S_A|=7$ exactly) and $S_B$ the complement of its
closed ball: $1+56=57$. For $t=1$: one color class of $B$ plus the other
fifteen classes of $A$ gives $4+60=64$ with both cells nonempty;
similarly for $t=2$ ($2+62$).
\end{proof}

The moral of Theorem~\ref{thm:rigid} is a warning: the ``odd overlap''
regime is not uniformly expensive. Odd $t=3$ carries a real rigidity
penalty; odd $t=1$ is combinatorially as cheap as the even case.

\subsection{Localization caps}

\begin{lemma}[$W_{\le6}$ localization]\label{lem:w6}
Any legal configuration whose occupied cells all lie inside a fixed point
set $W$ with $|W|\le 6$ has at most $64$ lines. \textsc{(proven)}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $D=[12]\setminus W$, $|D|=12-|W|\ge 6$. Every occupied cell $A\subseteq
W$ has support $H\supseteq D$. Classify lines by the restriction of their
sign vector to $D$ modulo global sign: $2^{|D|-1}$ classes, and (as in
Theorem~\ref{thm:structure}) each cell holds exactly $2^{8-|D|-1}$ lines
per class. Two same-class lines in \emph{distinct} cells $A,B\subseteq W$
conflict outright: their common support $R\supseteq D$ has
$|R\setminus D|=|W|-|A\cup B|\le 6-5=1$, and the restriction sum over $D$
is $\pm|D|$, so $\bigl|\sum_R\varepsilon\varepsilon'\bigr|\ge|D|-1\ge5$.
Hence each class contributes lines of at most one cell, i.e.\ at most
$2^{8-|D|-1}$ lines, and the total is at most
$2^{|D|-1}\cdot2^{8-|D|-1}=64$. One full cell attains it.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[$W_7$ localization]\label{lem:w7}
Any legal configuration whose occupied cells lie inside a fixed $7$-point
set has at most $64$ lines, attained. \textsc{(machine}: with
$|D|=5$ the same-class argument no longer gives outright conflict, but
dropping cross-class edges only relaxes the problem; the $16$ fold classes
on $D$ (each an exact $140$-vertex sub-problem over the $35$ cells) have
exact maximum independent sets of size $4$ each, summing to $64$
(\texttt{stage7.py}, \texttt{w7\_result.json}; about two seconds)%
\textsc{)}
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[$W_8$ localization]\label{lem:w8}
Any legal configuration whose occupied cells lie inside a fixed $8$-point
set $W$ has at most $128$ lines, attained. \textsc{(proven} from
Lemma~\ref{lem:w7}\textsc{)}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$W$ has eight $7$-point subsets $W'$; a cell $A\subseteq W$ ($|A|=4$) lies
in exactly $4$ of them (choose which of the $4$ points of
$W\setminus A$ to delete). Summing the Lemma~\ref{lem:w7} bound over the
eight $W'$ counts every line exactly $4$ times: $4N\le 8\cdot 64$, i.e.\
$N\le128$. Attained by a complementary pair of cells $A$ and $W\setminus
A$ ($t=0$, conflict-free): $64+64$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[full-cell collapse]\label{cor:fullcell}
If some cell is full ($n_A=64$) then every cell meeting $A$ is empty, all
other occupied cells lie inside the $8$-set $H_A$, and the configuration
has at most $64+128=192$ lines. Consequently every configuration with
$\ge193$ lines --- in particular any hypothetical $289$ --- has
$n_A\le 63$ in every cell. \textsc{(proven)}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Corollary~\ref{cor:kappa}, $n_A=64$ forces $n_B=0$ for every $B$ with
$B\cap A\ne\emptyset$; the surviving cells satisfy $B\cap A=\emptyset$,
i.e.\ $B\subseteq H_A$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:w8} caps them at $128$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The even side: classification, closure, and the equivalence}

Call a family of cells \emph{even} if all pairwise intersections
$|A\cap B|$ are even (i.e.\ $t\in\{0,2\}$ --- support overlaps
$|H\cap H'|\in\{4,6\}$). The slice-aligned cell families of
Theorem~\ref{thm:slice} are even, but the converse fails, and this failure
is precisely the hole in our earlier roadmap. The classification is,
pleasingly, the $d/e_7/e_8$ trichotomy familiar from doubly-even binary
codes and root lattices.

\begin{theorem}[even classification]\label{thm:evenclass}
Let $\mathcal F$ be an even family of cells, and make $\mathcal F$ a graph
by joining $A\sim B$ when $|A\cap B|=2$. Then the members of each
connected component $\mathcal F_0$, with point support
$P=\bigcup\mathcal F_0$, form one of:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(d)}] a \emph{pair-aligned} family: $P$ partitions into
$2$-point blocks such that every member of $\mathcal F_0$ is a union of
two blocks;
\item[\textup{(e7)}] a subfamily of the $7$ complements of the lines of a
Fano plane on $P$, $|P|=7$;
\item[\textup{(e8)}] a subfamily of the $14$ weight-$4$ codewords of an
extended-Hamming-type $[8,4,4]$ code on $P$, $|P|=8$.
\end{itemize}
Moreover the supports of distinct components are disjoint.
\textsc{(proven)}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Disjointness of component supports: if $x\in P_1\cap P_2$ then $x\in A\cap
B$ for some $A\in\mathcal F_1$, $B\in\mathcal F_2$, forcing $|A\cap B|=2$
and an edge across components.

Fix a component $\mathcal F_0$. Say a member $C$ \emph{splits} the block
$p=A\cap B$ (where $|p|=2$) if $|C\cap p|=1$.

\emph{Case 1: no member splits any block.} We claim the pairwise
intersections form a disjoint block family. Suppose $p=A\cap B$ and
$q=C\cap D$ with $|p\cap q|=1$, say $p=\{x,y\}$, $q=\{x,z\}$, $y\ne z$.
Since $C\supseteq q\ni x$ and $C$ does not split $p$, also $y\in C$;
likewise $y\in D$; but then $\{x,y,z\}\subseteq C\cap D=q$, impossible.
So distinct blocks are disjoint. Each member $A$ meets other members in
blocks contained in $A$, pairwise disjoint, hence in $0$, $1$ or $2$ whole
blocks; the remaining $4-2(\#\text{blocks in }A)$ points of $A$ lie in no
other member and can be grouped into fresh blocks arbitrarily. The result
is a pairing of $P$ with every member a union of two blocks: type (d).

\emph{Case 2: some $C$ splits $p=A\cap B=\{x,y\}$, with $x\in C$,
$y\notin C$.} Evenness forces $|C\cap A|=2$, say $C\cap A=\{x,a\}$ with
$a\in A\setminus p$, and $|C\cap B|=\{x,b\}$ with $b\in B\setminus p$; the
fourth point $c$ of $C$ lies outside $A\cup B$. Writing $A=\{x,y,a,a'\}$,
$B=\{x,y,b,b'\}$, $C=\{x,a,b,c\}$, the triple spans exactly $7$ points
$S=\{x,y,a,a',b,b',c\}$. Over $\mathbb F_2$, the span
$V_3=\langle A,B,C\rangle$ has dimension $3$ and its $7$ nonzero words
\[
A,\;B,\;C,\;A{+}B=\{a,a',b,b'\},\;A{+}C=\{y,a',b,c\},\;
B{+}C=\{y,b',a,c\},\;A{+}B{+}C=\{y,a',b',c\}
\]
all have weight $4$. A $[7,3]$ code with all nonzero weights $4$ is the
Fano-complement code: complements of the words are $3$-sets that pairwise
meet in exactly $|v\cap w|-1=1$ point (evenness forces $|v\cap w|=2$), and
$7\cdot\binom32=21=\binom72$ pairs means every point pair lies on exactly
one $3$-set --- a Steiner $S(2,3,7)$, i.e.\ a Fano plane on $S$, its lines
the complements. (Such classifications of codes generated by weight-$4$
words go back to \cite{PlessSloane75}.)

Now take any further member $D$ of $\mathcal F_0$. Evenness against
$A,B,C$ makes $\mathbf 1_D$ orthogonal to $V_3$, so $D\cap S\in
V_3^{\perp}\!\restriction_S=V_3\oplus\langle\mathbf 1_S\rangle$, whose
weights are $\{0,4\}\cup\{3,7\}$; since $|D|=4$, either $D\subseteq S$ and
$D\in V_3$ (a Fano complement), or $|D\cap S|=3$ with $D\cap S$ a Fano
line $L$ and $D=L\cup\{z\}$, $z\notin S$, or $D\cap S=\emptyset$. A member
disjoint from $S$ has intersection of size $\le1$ with every member
meeting $S$, so no edge connects it to them, and connectivity confines
$\mathcal F_0$ to members meeting $S$.

If no member takes the $L\cup\{z\}$ form, all members lie in $S$: type
(e7). Otherwise fix $D=L\cup\{z\}$ and set $S'=S\cup\{z\}$,
$V_4=\langle V_3,D\rangle$. For a Fano complement $v=S\setminus L'$:
$|v\cap D|=|v\cap L|=3-|L\cap L'|$, which is $2$ for $L'\ne L$ (weight of
$v+D$ is $4$) and $0$ for $L'=L$ (then $v+D=\mathbf 1_{S'}$). So $V_4$ is
a $[8,4]$ code on $S'$ containing $\mathbf 1_{S'}$ with all other nonzero
words of weight $4$ --- an extended-Hamming-type $[8,4,4]$ code, self-dual
and doubly even, with exactly $14$ weight-$4$ words. Any further member
$E$ is orthogonal to $V_4$, so $E\cap S'\in V_4^{\perp}=V_4$ with
$|E\cap S'|\in\{0,4\}$: either $E$ is one of the $14$ codewords
($E\subseteq S'$), or $E\cap S'=\emptyset$ --- and in the latter case $E$
again meets every $S'$-member in $\le1<2$ points, so connectivity excludes
it. Note also that a second splitting member $D'=L'\cup\{z'\}$ with
$z'\ne z$ is impossible at this stage, since $|D'\cap S'|=3\notin\{0,4\}$.
Hence all members lie in $S'$: type (e8).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[even closure]\label{thm:evenclosure}
Every legal configuration whose occupied cells form an even family has at
most $288$ lines, and any such configuration with $288$ lines is
slice-aligned: its occupied cells are $P$-aligned for some pairing $P$.
The branch caps are: pair-aligned $\le288$; a component of type (e7)
confines the whole configuration to $\le 128$ lines; type (e8) to
$\le192$. \textsc{(proven}, given the solver-certified (CP-SAT
\textsc{optimal}) slice cap of
Theorem~\ref{thm:slice}(b) and the \textsc{machine} caps of
Lemmas~\ref{lem:w7}--\ref{lem:w8}\textsc{)}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Decompose the occupied cells into components as in
Theorem~\ref{thm:evenclass}; supports are disjoint, and cells of distinct
components are conflict-free ($t=0$), so the configuration is a free union
over components.

If some component is of type (e7) with support $S$ ($7$ points): its lines
are capped by Lemma~\ref{lem:w7} at $64$. All other occupied cells lie in
the remaining $5$ points, which host at most one cell (two distinct
$4$-subsets of a $5$-set meet in $3$ points --- odd --- contradicting
evenness), for at most $64$ more: total $\le 128$.

If some component is of type (e8) with support $S'$ ($8$ points):
Lemma~\ref{lem:w8} caps it at $128$; the remaining $4$ points host at most
the single cell $[12]\setminus S'$: total $\le 192$. (Two components of
types e7/e8 cannot coexist: $7+7,7+8,8+8>12$.)

Otherwise every component is pair-aligned. Their pairings live on disjoint
supports; extend the union of the pairings arbitrarily to a full pairing
$P$ of $[12]$ (the leftover point count is even). Every occupied cell is
then $P$-aligned, and Theorem~\ref{thm:slice}(b) caps the configuration at
$288$. Since $128,192<288$, a $288$-line even configuration must be of
this slice-aligned kind.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[the odd pair is unavoidable]\label{cor:oddpair}
Every legal half-integer configuration with at least $289$ lines contains
two occupied cells with odd overlap ($t\in\{1,3\}$, support overlap
$|H\cap H'|\in\{5,7\}$). \textsc{(proven}, given the CP-SAT-\textsc{optimal}
slice cap of Theorem~\ref{thm:slice}(b)\textsc{)}
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[equivalence]\label{cor:equiv}
The odd-overlap lemma (``every legal configuration containing an occupied
odd-overlap cell pair has at most $288$ lines'') is \emph{equivalent} to
$\alpha(U)=288$. \textsc{(proven}, given the CP-SAT-\textsc{optimal}
slice cap of Theorem~\ref{thm:slice}(b)\textsc{)}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If the lemma holds, take any configuration with $\ge289$ lines: by the
lemma its occupied cells form an even family, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:evenclosure} caps it at $288$ --- contradiction; with the
$288$ witness, $\alpha(U)=288$. The converse is trivial.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[a repaired hole in the roadmap]\label{rem:roadmap}
Our campaign roadmap (artifact \texttt{K2\_novelty.json}, consequences
field --- a campaign artifact, not shipped with this note and distinct
from the shipped \texttt{certs/novelty.json}) asserted at cell level that a configuration escaping every slice
must contain an odd-overlap cell pair. That assertion is \emph{false}: the
e7 and e8 families of Theorem~\ref{thm:evenclass} are even families
aligned with no pairing --- cell-level counterexamples. The even-closure
theorem repairs the roadmap: what is true is not that even families are
slice-aligned, but that the non-slice-aligned even families (e7/e8) are
\emph{small} --- capped at $128/192$ lines, far below $288$. An earlier
draft of this note flagged the slice-like claim as an unproven heuristic;
Theorem~\ref{thm:evenclosure} replaces it.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The LP barrier: why cut aggregation cannot finish}

The ladder (Section~\ref{sec:ladder}), the pair caps, the localization
caps, the triple caps, and the slice cuts are all \emph{valid occupancy
inequalities}: linear inequalities $\sum_A c_A n_A\le b$ satisfied by the
occupancy vector of every legal configuration. It is natural to hope that
some finite library of such certified facts, fed to an LP, proves
$\alpha(U)\le 288$. That hope is provably dead:

\begin{theorem}[LP barrier]\label{thm:barrier}
Let $\mathcal I$ be \emph{any} family of valid occupancy inequalities in
which each member has at most $109$ cells with positive coefficient. Then
the linear program $\max\sum_A n_A$ subject to $\mathcal I$ and the box
constraints $0\le n_A\le 64$ has optimum at least
$31{,}680/109>290.6$. In particular no such family certifies
$\alpha(U)\le 289$, let alone $288$ --- where a family \emph{certifies}
$\alpha(U)\le b$ if its LP optimum is below $b+1$, so that flooring the
optimum proves the integer bound (the convention of the $\vartheta$
ceilings of Section~\ref{sec:sweep}); an optimum above $290.6$ leaves
every $b\le 289$ unreachable. \textsc{(proven)}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the uniform fractional point $x_A\equiv 64/109$, which satisfies
the box constraints. Let $\sum_A c_A n_A\le b$ be a valid inequality whose
positive support $\mathcal S=\{A:c_A>0\}$ has $|\mathcal S|\le109$ (if
$\mathcal S=\emptyset$, validity on the empty configuration gives $b\ge0$
and the point trivially satisfies it). For each $A\in\mathcal S$ the
single-cell configuration ``cell $A$ full'' is legal, so $64\,c_A\le b$;
averaging over $\mathcal S$,
\[
\sum_A c_A x_A\;\le\;\sum_{A\in\mathcal S}c_A\,\frac{64}{109}
\;\le\;\sum_{A\in\mathcal S}c_A\,\frac{64}{|\mathcal S|}
\;=\;\frac1{|\mathcal S|}\sum_{A\in\mathcal S}64\,c_A\;\le\;b .
\]
So $x$ is feasible, with objective $495\cdot64/109=31{,}680/109\approx
290.64$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:barriercuts}
Every cut family in this note has positive support far below the
threshold: pair cuts ($2$ cells), triple cuts ($3$), $W_{\le6}/W_7$
localization ($\le35$), e7/e8 cuts ($7/14$), slice cuts ($15$), ladder
cuts through level $3$ ($\le18$). An LP over all cuts of support $\le35$
has optimum $\ge 31{,}680/35>905$; adding the $W_8$ cuts (support $70$)
still leaves $\ge452$. Any LP/cutting-plane proof of $\alpha(U)\le288$
must use valid inequalities whose positive support covers at least $110$
of the $495$ cells --- and since $31{,}680/110=288$ exactly, support-$110$
families are the theoretical minimum. \textsc{(proven)}
\end{corollary}

Two honest remarks. First, the barrier binds the \emph{LP aggregation}
paradigm: a combinatorial case analysis may of course still use
small-support facts (the ladder's own Theorem~\ref{thm:ladder} is such a
statement, and complete orbit enumeration to the diameter would still
constitute a proof --- the barrier says the enumeration cannot be
short-circuited by an LP). Second, the barrier explains
Table~\ref{tab:loose}'s third row structurally: the $10{,}395$ slice cuts
have support $15$ each, so no reweighting of them could ever certify
anything below $31{,}680/15=2112$; their LP value $9504$ is not a solver
failure but a theorem-level impossibility.

\subsection{The star reduction: a global inequality that passes the
barrier}\label{sec:star}

The barrier demands valid inequalities with large support. There is a
natural family: one inequality per \emph{coordinate}.

For $x\in[12]$ let the \emph{star} of $x$ be the set of lines whose cell
(defect $4$-set) contains $x$: $\mathrm{star}_x=\{(A,u):x\in A\}$,
comprising the $\binom{11}3=165$ cells through $x$ and
$165\cdot64=10{,}560$ lines. Define the \emph{$U_{11}$ world} as the
half-integer world on ground set $[11]$: cells the $165$ three-subsets
$A'$ of $[11]$ (defects of $8$-point supports), $64$ even-sign lines per
cell, conflict iff $\bigl|\sum_{i\in H\cap H'}\varepsilon_i
\varepsilon'_i\bigr|\ge5$.

\begin{theorem}[star reduction]\label{thm:star}
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(i)}] For every $x$, the induced conflict graph on
$\mathrm{star}_x$ is isomorphic to the $U_{11}$ world via
$A\mapsto A\setminus\{x\}$: the conflict predicate never reads the
coordinate $x$. In $U_{11}$ every cell pair has common support
$r=5+|A'\cap B'|\in\{5,6,7\}$ --- there are \emph{no} free cell pairs, so
the pair cap $n_{A'}+n_{B'}\le64$ binds on all
$\binom{165}2$ pairs, and the entire cut library of this section
transfers verbatim. \textsc{(proven)}
\item[\textup{(ii)}] Every line lies in exactly $4$ stars, so for any
legal configuration $S$,
$4|S|=\sum_{x\in[12]}|S\cap\mathrm{star}_x|\le12\,\alpha(U_{11})$, i.e.
\[
\alpha(U_{12})\;\le\;3\,\alpha(U_{11}).
\]
\textsc{(proven)}
\item[\textup{(iii)}] The $288$-line witness of Theorem~\ref{thm:exists}
meets every star in exactly $96$ lines. \textsc{(machine}: recomputed from
the shipped witness certificate by \texttt{make verify-structure};
uniform $96\times12$\textsc{)} Consequently $\alpha(U_{11})\ge96$, and the
reduction (ii) is exactly tight on the witness: $4\cdot288=12\cdot96$.
\item[\textup{(iv)}] If $\alpha(U_{11})\le 96$ then $\alpha(U_{12})=288$:
the odd-overlap lemma holds, the ladder's gap closes, and the
$W_2\cup U$ architecture is globally sealed at $420$ lines.
\textsc{(proven}, given (ii) and the witness\textsc{)}
\item[\textup{(v)}] Conversely, by pigeonhole any $289$-line
configuration meets some star in at least
$\lceil 4\cdot289/12\rceil=97$ lines: \emph{every} record attempt in the
half-integer world contains a $97$-line $U_{11}$ configuration.
\textsc{(proven)}
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i) A cell $A\ni x$ has support $H=[12]\setminus A\subseteq
[12]\setminus\{x\}$; identifying $[12]\setminus\{x\}$ with $[11]$, cells
through $x$ correspond bijectively to $3$-subsets $A'=A\setminus\{x\}$,
supports and sign classes are preserved, and the conflict predicate
depends only on $H\cap H'\subseteq[11]$ and the sign vectors. For the
overlap census: $|A'\cap B'|\in\{0,1,2\}$ gives
$r=11-|A'\cup B'|=5+|A'\cap B'|\ge5$. (ii) $|A|=4$: a line's cell contains
exactly $4$ coordinates, so the line lies in exactly $4$ stars; each
$S\cap\mathrm{star}_x$ is independent in the $\mathrm{star}_x$ world,
hence of size $\le\alpha(U_{11})$ by (i). (iii) is a finite recomputation
from \texttt{certs/config420.json}. (iv) $288\le\alpha(U_{12})
\le3\cdot96=288$. (v) If all twelve stars held $\le96$, the sum
$4\cdot289=1156>12\cdot96=1152$ fails.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[the star cuts pass the barrier]
Each star cut $\sum_{A\ni x}n_A\le\alpha(U_{11})$ has positive support
$165\ge110$ cells --- the first valid family we possess on the far side of
Theorem~\ref{thm:barrier} --- and if the cap $96$ holds, the twelve star
cuts alone close the world: $4\sum_A n_A=\sum_x\sum_{A\ni x}n_A
\le12\cdot96$ gives exactly $288$. The uniform witness saturation (iii)
shows they are tight, so no slack is wasted: the star family is not
merely sufficient, it is exactly calibrated.
\end{remark}

The section's conclusion is a genuine reduction of both open questions of
this note to one:

\begin{problem}[the $U_{11}$ problem]\label{prob:u11}
Is $\alpha(U_{11})\le 96$? Equivalently (given the witness): does the
$10{,}560$-line, $165$-cell $U_{11}$ world --- three times smaller than
$U_{12}$, with no free cell pairs --- have independence number exactly
$96$? A proof closes $\alpha(U_{12})=288$ and hence the odd-overlap lemma
and the $420$-line global cap (Theorem~\ref{thm:star}(iv)); a $97$-line
$U_{11}$ witness would refute the star route and is a \emph{necessary}
ingredient of any $842$-point record in this architecture
(Theorem~\ref{thm:star}(v)), though not by itself sufficient.
\textsc{(open)}
\end{problem}

\section{The architecture sweep: every enumerable frame family closes

The slice-closure results seal one architecture. In parallel we ran a
closing sweep over every other frame architecture we could enumerate, for
both record targets ($303$ lines $=606$ points in $\RR^{11}$, beating
$604$; $421$ lines $=842$ points in $\RR^{12}$, beating $841$). Each
candidate family is compiled to an exact conflict graph over its ring
($\QQ(\sqrt2)$ or $\QQ(\sqrt5)$; the adjudicator works over $\QQ(\sqrt d)$
for square-free $d$ and passes an adversarial self-test), then closed by
one of: exact ILP duality (HiGHS \cite{HiGHS18}, integer optimal), or a
\emph{certified} Lov\'asz $\vartheta$ bound \cite{Lovasz79}. The
$\vartheta$ certification works as follows
(\texttt{kissing\_frame\_adjudicate.py}, a campaign tool that is not part
of this repository): the dual
$\vartheta(G)=\min_Y\lambda_{\max}(J+Y)$ ($Y$ symmetric, supported on
edges) is solved numerically; $Y$ is then rounded to dyadic rationals
(denominator $2^{16}$) so that $B=J+Y$ is \emph{exactly} representable in
binary floating point, and a dyadic $t_{\mathrm{cert}}\ge
\lambda_{\max}(B)$ is certified by a shifted floating-point Cholesky
factorization of $t_{\mathrm{cert}}I-B$: the factorization succeeds with a
shift of $2^{-16}$, which exceeds $100$ times the Demmel--Rump rounding
radius $\gamma\lVert M\rVert_\infty$, $\gamma=(n{+}1)u/(1-(n{+}1)u)$,
$u=2^{-53}$, so $\lambda_{\min}(t_{\mathrm{cert}}I-B)>0$ rigorously (up to
IEEE-754 conformance of the hardware). Since any such $B$ is a feasible
$\vartheta$ dual matrix, $\alpha(G)\le\vartheta(G)\le t_{\mathrm{cert}}$,
and the stated integer ceiling $\lfloor t_{\mathrm{cert}}\rfloor$ is a
theorem for the graph, not a solver report.

\section{Discussion and open problems}\label{sec:disc}

\emph{What is new.} A second, provably non-isometric $420$-line antipodal
configuration in $\RR^{12}$; the first (to our knowledge) built on partial
cells, found by unbiased search over a mixed-shell world and then fully
explained by an exact rotation certificate; slice-closure theorems that
cap its entire architecture at the record tie; a machine-checked exclusion
ladder localizing where any improvement must live; an exact structure
theory for the half-integer world (pair structure lemma, even closure, the
equivalence of Corollary~\ref{cor:equiv}); an LP barrier theorem
explaining why the cut paradigm cannot finish; a star reduction that
collapses the frontier to the $U_{11}$ world; and a certified sweep that
seals the enumerable alternatives.

\emph{What is honestly open.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The $U_{11}$ problem (headline): is $\alpha(U_{11})\le96$?}
(Problem~\ref{prob:u11}.) By Theorem~\ref{thm:star} this single question
now carries both halves of the frontier: a proof gives
$\alpha(U_{12})=288$ --- the odd-overlap lemma
(Corollary~\ref{cor:equiv}), the global $420$-line cap for the
$W_2\cup U$ architecture, and the retirement of the ladder's gap --- while
a $97$-line $U_{11}$ witness is a necessary ingredient of any $842$-point
record in this architecture. The $U_{11}$ world is three times smaller
than $U_{12}$ ($10{,}560$ lines, $165$ cells), has no free cell pairs, and
inherits the entire certified cut library of Section~\ref{sec:odd}
($\kappa=64$ on every pair, $t{=}3$ rigidity $57$, localization
$64/64/128$, sunflower $80$'s, e7/e8 caps $64/128$). The natural attack is
two-sided: a KaMIS lower hunt for $97$, and a CP-SAT ladder on $U_{11}$
seeded with those cuts --- mindful that Theorem~\ref{thm:barrier} (which
applies verbatim to $U_{11}$: certifying $\alpha(U_{11})\le96$ requires
positive support at least $\lceil 10{,}560/97\rceil=109$ of the $165$
cells) again forbids a small-support LP finish there.
\item \textbf{The odd-overlap lemma, now an equivalence.} Prove: every
legal half-integer configuration containing a cell pair with
$|H\cap H'|\in\{5,7\}$ has at most $288$ lines. Section~\ref{sec:odd}
proved this is not merely sufficient but \emph{equivalent} to
$\alpha(U)=288$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:equiv}), and that any $289$-line
configuration must contain an occupied odd pair
(Corollary~\ref{cor:oddpair}). The barrier theorem constrains the proof
shape: no aggregation of the small-support facts we hold can do it; the
star route (Problem~\ref{prob:u11}) is the one global inequality family
known to be both valid and tight.
\item \textbf{Orbit enumeration at levels $4$ and $5$.} Quadruple orbits
are within reach ($18$--$21$-cell universes solve to \textsc{optimal} in
minutes), and each further level shrinks the search region for a $289$;
complete enumeration to the diameter would still constitute a proof.
Theorem~\ref{thm:barrier} tempers the ambition: bounded-level ladder
certificates are support-$\le(15{+}k)$ cuts, so no LP over them closes the
world --- the enumeration must actually reach exhaustion, or be replaced
by the $U_{11}$ route.
\item \textbf{Global $\alpha(U)$ relaxations.} Is there any tractable
relaxation adapted to the $64$-per-cell-pair conflict structure that beats
the gaps of Table~\ref{tab:loose}? The barrier theorem sharpens the
question: any useful valid-inequality family must have $\ge110$-cell
positive support. The twelve star cuts are the first such family; finding
a \emph{second}, independent one (weighted stars? spectral cuts on the
$U_{11}$ quotient?) would over-determine the polytope near the witness and
might close the gap without solving Problem~\ref{prob:u11} exactly.
\item \textbf{The $842$ frontier.} An antipodal $842$-point configuration
means $421$ lines at coherence $\tfrac12$; the LP ceiling is $614$ lines
\cite{RussellLines26}, so geometry leaves $46\%$ headroom, while every
architecture in this note and its predecessors closes at $\le420$. The
enumerable-frame space is exhausted, not the problem: the biquadratic
$\QQ(\sqrt2,\sqrt5)$ hybrid sector (icosahedral frames mixed with root
alphabets) is untested for lack of exact tooling, and a closed-form
negative theorem for the weight-$4$-based design space (``no such
architecture reaches the target'') is supported by every data point but
unproven. Within the half-integer route specifically, the record hunt is
now the $97$-in-$U_{11}$ hunt of Problem~\ref{prob:u11}.
\item \textbf{Classification at $420$.} Are the classical configuration
and the configuration of Theorem~\ref{thm:exists} the only $420$-line
systems in $\RR^{12}$ up to isometry? The rotation certificate shows both
live in the integer world up to isometry; a classification of maximum
antipodal configurations there (whole and partial cells) may be within
reach of the counting method, now supplemented by the even-classification
trichotomy of Theorem~\ref{thm:evenclass}.
\end{enumerate}

\emph{Relation to the record.} Nothing in this note improves $k(12)\ge841$
\cite{TAY26}; the new configuration ties the \emph{antipodal} record of
$840$ points with a second, inequivalent object, and the closure theorems
say that this entire corner of the landscape is now understood exactly:
$421$ needs either odd-overlap mixing in the half-integer world ---
provably unavoidable (Corollary~\ref{cor:oddpair}), against the ladder's
evidence, and now concentrated in the single question of
Problem~\ref{prob:u11} --- or an architecture nobody has written down
yet.

\section{Postscript (v1.1, July 12, 2026): Problem~\ref{prob:u11} is
answered in the negative}\label{sec:postscript}

Everything above this section is v1.0 as published --- theorems, solver
labels and \textsc{(open)} markers unchanged. This dated postscript records
what a subsequent large-scale computation settled, and what it did not.

\begin{theorem}[$\alpha(U_{11})\ge98$]\label{thm:u11witness}
The $U_{11}$ world contains an independent set of $98$ lines (and at least
five further, pairwise-distinct independent sets of $97$ lines). Hence
$\alpha(U_{11})\ge98>96$: Problem~\ref{prob:u11} is answered in the
negative, and the twelve star cuts cannot close $\alpha(U_{12})=288$.
\textsc{(machine}: witnesses shipped, verified by
\texttt{make verify-u11}\textsc{)}
\end{theorem}

The witnesses were found by independent KaMIS lotteries on an exactly
generated $U_{11}$ conflict graph whose generator was validated against
this note's own structure facts before any search ran: $165$ cells,
$10{,}560$ lines, cell-pair census $4{,}620/6{,}930/1{,}980$ at overlaps
$r=5/6/7$ with exactly $256/128/512$ conflict edges per pair
(Lemma-level censuses of Section~\ref{sec:odd}), uniform degree $584$,
$3{,}083{,}520$ edges in total. The certificates
(\texttt{certs/u11\_witness98.json}, \texttt{certs/u11\_witnesses97.json})
store only raw line descriptions --- a cell $3$-subset plus a canonical
even sign pattern per line, no counts, no verdicts; the stdlib checker
\texttt{scripts/verify\_u11.py} rebuilds every integer representative
($\pm1$ on the $8$-support, norm$^2=8$) and recomputes every pairwise
inner product exactly (conflict iff the signed shared sum on the support
overlap has absolute value $\ge5$, i.e.\ $4\langle g,g'\rangle^2>64$ ---
the predicate of Section~\ref{sec:star} verbatim), in the same
recompute-never-echo discipline as the v1.0 checkers; the adversarial
selftest battery grows to $26$ cases accordingly.

\emph{Upper side, honestly labeled.} A CP-SAT optimization on the same
graph was budget-terminated at status \textsc{feasible} with incumbent
$98$ and internal dual bound $144$. That bound is \emph{solver-reported
and uncertified} --- no \textsc{optimal} status, no exact dual
certificate, so unlike the receipts of v1.0 it does not even reach
receipt grade. The honest state of knowledge is
\[
98\;\le\;\alpha(U_{11})\;\le\;144\quad\text{(upper end solver-reported,
uncertified)}.
\]

\emph{Consequences for the frontier.} Theorem~\ref{thm:star} is
unaffected, but the hypothesis of its item (iv) is now known to be false,
so the star route to global closure is dead and the reduction weakens to
$\alpha(U_{12})\le3\cdot98=294$. Item (v)'s necessary condition for any
$842$-point record is now known to be \emph{satisfiable}: $\ge97$-line
$U_{11}$ configurations exist. The record question therefore turns
entirely on extendability --- and there the evidence is sharply negative.
Each of the six lottery-optimal stars was mapped back into $U_{12}$
(exact inverse of the star restriction of Theorem~\ref{thm:star}(i),
every image re-verified as a $U_{12}$ independent set) and its full
compatible remainder computed \emph{exactly} (the remainders are small
enough that no solver is involved): two of the $97$s extend by $0$ lines,
two by $1$, one by $3$, and the $98$-line witness by $1$ --- best total
$100$ lines in $U_{12}$, against the $289$ a record needs. The shipped
$288$-line witness behaves in the opposite way: its (only-$96$-line)
star leaves a $224$-line compatible remainder that contains its entire
$192$-line complement. \emph{Lottery-optimal stars are maximally jammed:
star density trades against global extendability.} (These six extension
computations are exact session results, reported here; they are not part
of this repository's certificate set.)

\emph{What remains open.} All empirical evidence continues to point at
$\alpha(U_{12})=288$: six independent KaMIS lotteries on the full
$23{,}569{,}920$-edge $U_{12}$ conflict graph each landed on exactly
$288$; a CP-SAT optimize warm-started at the $288$-line witness never
improved it; the exact ladder of Theorem~\ref{thm:ladder} and every orbit
extension tested net negative. But with the star route closed and the
barrier of Theorem~\ref{thm:barrier} still standing,
$\alpha(U_{12})=288$ --- and with it the odd-overlap lemma and the global
$420$-line cap --- remains \emph{open}. Problem~\ref{prob:u11} as posed
is settled; the frontier question it leaves behind is: \emph{does any
$\ge97$-line $U_{11}$ configuration extend to $289$ lines in $U_{12}$?}
